Бюллетень кинопрокатчика (2003–2008)

The Byulleten Kinoprokatchika was Russia’s only weekly trade publication for cinema exhibitors. Before it, there was only Kinobusiness.com — oriented toward future collaborations with The Hollywood Reporter and Variety, with no editorial policy for exhibitors. We became Deadline for the Russian industry: getting the numbers in rubles from the distributors, communicating with the theatres, interviewing arthouse and major studio executives, publishing box-office forecasts and personal opinions on commercial prospects. Nobody had done that in Russia before us.

The publication was created in late November 2003. I joined at issue four as a translator. Its founder, Ruslan Tatarintsev, left for UIP Russia in February 2004, leaving the entire editorial operation to me. Over the next four years I built the publication’s analytical vocabulary, assembled its team from film critics and cinema managers, developed its forecasting methodology — now deployed by Fond Kino, Kinoprime, and Kinometro itself — and maintained a state-critical editorial voice until the project was acquired by a state-adjacent marketing agency that still owns it. The publication continues with only one mention of me as the first editor. The vocabulary I introduced is still in use. The methodology I built disappeared when I did. This page, compiled from the archive of 2003–2008 finally obtained in March 2026, presents the hard evidence.

The Team I Built

I recruited film critics and cinema professionals from press screenings, personal contacts, and the cinema exhibition circuit. Under my editorship, circulation grew from 900 to 5,000 copies. The operation expanded to include a bilingual English edition for Cannes (May 2007), UIP NEWS newsletters (9 issues, commissioned by Universal/Paramount JV), and Современный Кинотеатр (24 issues for cinema exhibitors). From 2007, Planet Inform brought BK into international rotation at film markets. I represented the Russian box-office numbers to the markets in English, as co-editor and co-writer of most of the international editions.

Predictive Analysis: The Sequelization Thesis

In January 2026, four fairy-tale adaptations earned €49 million in three days in Russian cinemas. The fairy tales outearned the latest war film by a factor of eighty. This is not a coincidence.

PhasePeriodMechanism
12022–2027Adapt every beloved fairy tale. Build audience association with Russian family cinema as warmth and nostalgia.
2NowSequelize. Source material is finite but franchises are established. Cheburashka 2 released January 2026.
318–24 monthsWhen source material exhausts, sequels create original plots. State narratives enter through characters audiences already welcome.

Precedent: Bogatyri (2004–present, 10+ films) and Ivan-Tsarevich (2011–present, 5+ films) started as folk-comedy animation and gradually absorbed patriotic, military, and state-aligned themes over two decades. The same pattern is now beginning in live-action family cinema at industrial scale. Cinema Fund budget: ~RUB 40B annually.

This analysis is original. No European institution currently monitors adversarial entertainment production as a dedicated threat domain.
